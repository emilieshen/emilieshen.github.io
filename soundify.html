soundify

Overview

As a part of COGS 187A: Information Architecture at UC San Diego, two other team mates and I designed a music app, using Spotify and SoundCloud as sources of inspiration for both well designed elements and elements that could use some redesign. We redesigned the experience and steps a user would go through when editing or creating a playlist and for discovering new music. We also redesigned the visual layout of some elements.

My Role

I worked with two other classmates on this project. We each interviewed a minimum of 4 individuals. We all analyzed these interviews and did research on other music streaming apps. We made comparisons between apps to show what was a good user experience/flow and what was a less good user experience/flow. We also created did an equal amount of work in competitive analysis. In terms of creating the prototype, we created initial sketches of each screen together. I worked on designs for half of the screens in Sketch to import into Invision, which can be viewed by clicking to test the prototype.

User Research

We conducted user interviews, asking people about their experience with music streaming apps that they use, and observing them as they performed certain tasks within their preferred app, such as creating a playlist, editing a playlist, and finding new music to listen to. We interviewed college students at UC San Diego. We asked them questions about their prefered music app in terms of when they listened to music, what they liked and disliked about the app, and their experience with creating and editing playlists. Through in person interviews and think-alouds, we observed that users have their own specific apps that they prefer for streaming and listening to music; Spotify and SoundCloud seem to be more preferable among our interviewees, however not by an extremely large margin. We also developed various scenarios for users to complete as a think-aloud exercise using their preferred app.


Competitive Analysis

We completed a competitive analysis, comparing other music streaming apps, such as Spotify, SoundCloud, Pandora, and Google Play Music.


Prototyping & Solution

We first created very quick prototypes with paper and pen. Because users found it difficult to edit their playlists, we decided to make this experience easier and simpler. In many apps, we found that if a user wanted to delete songs from their playlist, they had to delete each song one at a time, which was very inefficient. Also, there was no error prevention in deleting songs. When a user clicked delete, there was no confirmation to the user on whether or not they wanted to complete the action of deleting that song. We changed this process by creating a checklist type of deletion system, where the user could select multiple songs they wanted to delete. We created a high fidelity prototype using Sketch and Invision. We thought about whether the user should be led back to the page they were on prior to creating a playlist and adding a song to that playlist, or if they should be directed to the newly created playlist. However, we did not want the user to be confused on where they were in terms of navigation, especially when visibility of system status is so important. We decided to keep the user on the screen that they were on prior to creating the playlist. For the navigation bar, it was difficult to choose what options to have. We chose to have Home, Explore, Search, Library, and Profile. SoundCloud includes browsing and search as combined, and Spotify includes browsing and search as separate features. We felt that it was important to keep these two features separate because these are for two different purposes. Browse is for finding new music to listen to and discovering, while search is used to find a specific artist or song. We included Profile as an option in the navigation bar, since we wanted to put some emphasis on the social aspect due to the findings from interviews as mentioned earlier.

Regarding the Library, we discussed what kind of content that we wanted to display on page and how it would be structured. At first, we only had recently played playlists appear on the page, but we decided that it would be more salient to expand the results and have “recently listened” music listed, whether it be playlists, stations, or artists, to provide more content. This gave the user more freedom to navigate and recall their most recent choices. Originally, we structured our “recent” information in a list format, but realized that using a grid format with larger thumbnails would be more coherent for users, especially for those that are attempting to multitask while listening to music. Many of our interviewees stated that they use music apps while driving, so choosing and pressing on a larger thumbnail with more space area is easier, which also applies to the way our actual playlist page is set up (grid). Additionally, the list items we ended up with were Likes, Playlists, Artists, Songs, and Radio. We came to this conclusion to limit the list items to 5 of the most used categories (according to our interviewees and ourselves, and ones similar across different apps). For editing tracks, we decided to show the process for deleting songs, since this was something all of us were having an issue with in every music app we found. In Spotify, the option to delete songs from a playlist was in a very unexpected place, and while it was where we expected it to be on SoundCloud, it was still a longer process than it had to be. We drew our inspiration from the delete option of the iMessage app, where selecting “Edit” would allow the user to choose which selections they wanted to delete, and then delete them all simultaneously with the press of one button; conversely, other music apps force users to delete items one by one. This follows a more intuitive, efficient process for the user because what would’ve taken them an excessive amount of steps in either SoundCloud or Spotify would only take them a few taps.
